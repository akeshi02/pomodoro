July 3, 1888
The Disappearing Lady
Case No. 12 - The case of the disappearing lady is quite perplexing. I was called to assist Inspector Lestrade at the Thames Embankment. The witness, Victor Watson, reported seeing a lady vanish behind a street lamp at 8:15 PM. She was wearing a distinctive blue riding habit and carried a pocket watch.
Case No. 12 - The witness also mentioned that the lady was "very anxious" about something, but couldn't elaborate. The man who was with her had a rather unusual scar on his right cheek. This seems to be a crucial detail.
Case No. 12 - Upon further examination, I found that the witness's account contains inconsistencies. The same man, Mr. Watson, was seen with the lady at 8:30 PM the previous evening, but with a different scar location (left cheek instead of right). This inconsistency suggests possible deception.
Case No. 12 - Additionally, the pipe of Mr. Watson contained traces of arsenic in the tobacco. This is a significant clue - arsenic was used in some theatrical performances to create the illusion of death.
Case No. 12 - Based on this evidence, I suspect the lady was performing a magic trick or illusion. The disappearance was a controlled event, not a genuine act of crime. The lady may have been a magician, and Mr. Watson was part of her act.
Case No. 12 - The lady was not harmed; she simply disappeared as part of her performance. I have advised Inspector Lestrade that this is an illusion, not a crime. The case has been closed.
